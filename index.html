Rebate Debate 87A

"A saga of Specific Exclusion to Exclusive Inclusion"

Really a question of fact?

What Does the Proviso to Section 87A Say?

"Provided that where the total income of the assessee is chargeable to tax under sub-section (1A) of section 115BAC‚Ä¶"

This specifically says that the 87A rebate applies only to incomes taxed (at slab rates) under Section 115BAC. The proviso makes it clear that the rebate is not meant for incomes subjected to special or flat rates outside the scope of 115BAC.

A Comparison of Pre- and Post-115BAC Amendments to 87A

Before the introduction of Section 115BAC, the 87A rebate was relatively straightforward:

"[87A. Rebate of income-tax in case of certain individuals.‚ÄîAn assessee, being an individual resident in India, whose total income does not exceed ‚Çπ5,00,000, shall be entitled to a deduction from the income-tax calculated on his total income, equal to 100% of the income-tax or ‚Çπ12,500, whichever is lower.]"

This version of 87A was not linked to income types and was more universally applicable for those within the income threshold. However, with the introduction of 115BAC, the scope of 87A was amended.

Previously, section 112A(6) explicitly excluded Long-Term Capital Gains (LTCG) under Section 112A from the purview of the 87A rebate. But post-amendment, 87A now applies only to incomes charged under 115BAC, which includes income taxed at slab rates.

Conclusion: A Shift from Specific Exclusion to Exclusive Inclusion

The confusion surrounding the rebate debate stems from a fundamental shift in the way 87A operates. The old provision used a system of specific exclusions, whereas the amended 87A uses an exclusive inclusion model, limiting the rebate to those incomes specifically covered under 115BAC.

The only oversight by the Income Tax Department, which remains unexplained, is its approval of the 87A rebate on flat-rate incomes before July 5th, 2024. On one hand, Beena Manishbhai Fofaria's (from Gujarat) appeal to receive the rebate for flat-rate incomes‚Äîcontrary to the amended 87A‚Äîwas upheld by the Additional/Joint Commissioner of Income Tax (Appeals) in Pune. On the other hand, the judgment on the Public Interest Litigation (PIL 32465 of 2024, in the High Court of Judicature at Bombay, Ordinary Original Civil Jurisdiction), seeking to restore the 87A rebate for all incomes except those under Section 112A (as it was before the amendment), is still pending as of the date this article is published.

It is awaited whether the department's stance holds up in light of the judgment. Otherwise, the department seems to be following the Act, though this may change after the judgment.
